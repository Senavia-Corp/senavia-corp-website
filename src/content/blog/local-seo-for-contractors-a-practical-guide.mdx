> **TL;DR** — For contractors, local SEO comes down to three things: a fully optimized Google Business Profile, a steady flow of recent reviews, and a website with clear location and service pages. Get those right and you'll show up when someone nearby searches "[your trade] near me."

When a homeowner needs a roofer, an electrician, or a remodeler, they don't open the Yellow Pages — they search Google. And the businesses that show up in that little map at the top of the results (the "map pack") get the lion's share of the calls. Local SEO is how you get there. This guide breaks it down into the moves that actually matter for contractors.

## Start with your Google Business Profile

Your Google Business Profile (GBP) is the single most important asset in local SEO. It's free, and it directly feeds the map pack. Yet most contractors leave it half-finished. To compete, fill out everything:

- **Accurate name, address, and phone number (NAP).** These must match your website and every other listing exactly — even small inconsistencies confuse Google.
- **Primary and secondary categories.** Choose the most specific category that fits ("Roofing Contractor," not just "Contractor").
- **Service areas.** List the cities and neighborhoods you actually serve.
- **Photos.** Real job-site photos, your team, your trucks. Profiles with photos get far more clicks and calls.
- **Services and descriptions.** List every service with a short, keyword-aware description.
- **Hours, including holidays.** Outdated hours quietly cost you trust.

Then keep it active. Post updates, answer questions, and respond to reviews. Google rewards profiles that look alive.

## Reviews are your ranking engine

For contractors, reviews do double duty: they influence where you rank *and* they're the deciding factor for the customer choosing between you and the next listing. A few principles:

- **Recency matters.** Ten reviews from this year beat fifty from three years ago. Aim for a steady trickle, not a one-time burst.
- **Ask every happy customer.** The best time is right after a job goes well. Make it easy — text them the direct review link.
- **Respond to all of them.** Thank the positive reviews and address the negative ones professionally. Future customers read your responses as closely as the reviews themselves.
- **Never buy fake reviews.** Google detects them, and the penalty isn't worth it.

## Build location and service pages on your website

The map pack is fed partly by your website's relevance. Two page types do the heavy lifting:

- **Service pages.** One dedicated page per major service — roofing, gutters, repairs — each genuinely written for that topic, not a thin paragraph.
- **Location pages.** If you serve multiple cities, a page for each (Fort Lauderdale, Pembroke Pines, Coral Springs) with real, specific content about your work there. Avoid copy-paste pages that just swap the city name — Google sees through that, and so do customers.

Each page should include your NAP, a clear call-to-action, and proof: photos, reviews, and specifics about the work.

## Get the technical basics right

You don't need to be a developer, but a few things must be in place:

1. **Mobile-first design.** Most local searches happen on phones. A site that's hard to use on mobile loses the job.
2. **Fast load times.** Slow pages bleed visitors. Compress images and keep things lean.
3. **Click-to-call buttons.** Make the phone number tappable on every page.
4. **Local business schema.** Structured data tells search engines exactly who you are, where, and what you do.

## Consistency across the web (citations)

Google cross-checks your business across directories — Yelp, Angi, the BBB, industry-specific sites. When your NAP is consistent everywhere, Google trusts the data more and ranks you higher. Audit your listings, fix mismatches, and claim any profiles you haven't.

## A realistic timeline

Local SEO is not an overnight switch. Expect to see real movement over two to four months of consistent effort, with results compounding from there. The contractors who win are the ones who treat it as an ongoing habit — a few reviews a week, profile updates, fresh photos — rather than a one-time project. Stay consistent, and local search becomes one of the most reliable, lowest-cost sources of jobs your business has.
